    I’m a senior data scientist at Hawkfish, Mike Bloomberg’s political tech and data organization, and have spent the last year working to defeat Donald Trump and win back the senate for the Democrats.
    At Hawkfish, my work has focused on voter engagement and persuasion: building voter models for use in audience generation, conducting randomized controlled trials and observational studies to identify the best ad content and settings, and Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) Modeling to identify which individuals are persuaded most strongly by particular ads.

    Example Projects from the Past Year

    • Bayesian Hypothesis Testing API As part of the Experiments team for the Mike Bloomberg 2020 Campaign, I built our hypothesis testing tool using a Bayesian approach. The team deployed sets of ads in a Randomized Controlled Trial setting and wanted to know which ad(s) drove the highest rates of conversion (clicks, email submission, form completion, etc.). The tool modeled conversion rates using the beta distribution, enabling fast Bayesian updating, and producing results that were easy to interpret by a broad audience (e.g. ‘probability of being best’ versus p-values). I deployed the API on kubernetes using flask.
    • Voter Modeling to Predict Registration, Voting By Mail, and Voting Early In the early summer I built a number of voter models to predict the probability of individuals (1) registering to vote in the coming months, (2) voting by mail, and (3) voting early, in order to inform audience generation and ad targeting. The models used the XGBoost gradient boosted trees algorithm to fit over 1,000 distinct voter features. I deployed the models on Hawkfish’s Kubeflow machine learning platform, using Katib for parallelized hyperparameter tuning, and Argo to score the country’s 257 million eligible voters. The voter registration scores were used by registration efforts that registered more voters in AZ, GA, MI, PA, and WI than were the margins of difference in those states.
    • Causal Inference of Observational Data with Bootstrapped Logistic Regression Causal inference is best done in a randomized controlled trial (RCT) setting, but there are times when a full RCT set up isn’t practical or possible. To assess ad performance as best we could outside of RCTs, I built a logistic regression framework that controlled for potentially confounding variables like viewer age, time of day, site domain, and other factors, and isolated the effect of ad content on engagement metrics like view completion and click through rates. I deployed the framework on Kubeflow which, given its ability to deploy hundreds of runs in parallel, enabled boostrap sampling to generate confidence intervals. Kubeflow also enabled running many conditional settings in parallel, so we could see how ads performed across audiences as a whole and also within different demographic buckets.
